## 2. Core Concepts – From Shor to Lattice in 60 Seconds

Quantum‑resistant cryptography (QRC) is often lumped together with “post‑quantum” as a buzzword, but the underlying mathematics matters. Below is a rapid‑fire primer that every CISO should be able to recite:

| Category | Representative Algorithms (2024‑25) | Hard Problem |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Lattice‑based** | `Kyber` (KEM), `Dilithium` (signature), `Falcon` (signature) | Shortest Vector Problem (SVP) & Learning With Errors (LWE) |
| **Code‑based** | `Classic McEliece` | Decoding random linear codes |
| **Hash‑based** | `SPHINCS+` | Merkle‑tree signatures, hash collisions |
| **Multivariate** | `GeMSS` (experimental) | Solving systems of multivariate quadratic equations |
| **Isogeny‑based** | *Withdrawn* (SIKE) | Supersingular isogeny graphs (now broken) |

*Why lattice?* Because the best known quantum attacks (e.g., quantum‑enhanced lattice reduction) still run in exponential time, making them the most practical choice for high‑throughput environments.

### The Shor Shockwave

In 1994, Peter Shor showed that a sufficiently large quantum computer could factor integers and compute discrete logarithms in polynomial time. RSA‑2048 and ECC‑P‑256, the workhorses of today’s TLS, rely precisely on those problems. The **quantum threat** is not “if” but “when,” and the “when” is rapidly moving from “decades away” to “within the next five years” according to the latest IBM Q‑Roadmap.

## 4. Standards – The Blueprint That Will Govern 2025

### 4.1 NIST’s Final Selection

| Algorithm | Function | Security Level (NIST) | Approx. Key/Signature Size |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `CRYSTALS‑Kyber` | Key‑Encapsulation Mechanism (KEM) | L1‑L5 (Kyber‑1024 = L3) | Ciphertext ≈ 1 KB, Public key ≈ 800 B |
| `CRYSTALS‑Dilithium` | Digital Signature | L1‑L5 (Dilithium‑3 = L3) | Signature ≈ 3 KB |
| `Falcon` | Digital Signature | L1‑L5 (Falcon‑1024 = L3) | Signature ≈ 900 B |
| `SPHINCS+` | Stateless Hash‑Based Signature | L1‑L5 (SPHINCS+‑256s = L3) | Signature ≈ 41 KB |

*FIPS 203* will codify these four primitives, providing a **single, interoperable standard** that all federal agencies—and by extension, their contractors—must follow. The standard also defines **parameter sets** for different security levels, allowing enterprises to pick the right trade‑off between size, speed, and security.

### 4.2 What the Standards Mean for You

1. **Interoperability** – No more “vendor‑lock” on a proprietary post‑quantum scheme.
2. **Compliance** – Passing a FIPS 140‑2/3 audit will automatically cover quantum‑resistance after 2025.
3. **Future‑Proofing** – The selected algorithms have survived **seven years** of public scrutiny, making them the safest bet for the next decade.

## 9. Actionable Checklist – What Every Enterprise Should Do Today

1. **Create a Crypto Asset Register** – List every RSA/ECC key, certificate, and signature used across the organization.
2. **Run a Hybrid TLS Test** – Deploy OpenSSL 3.2 with Kyber on a non‑critical service; measure latency and CPU.
3. **Engage Vendors Early** – Ask HSM and PKI providers for FIPS 203 roadmaps; secure early‑access firmware.
4. **Update CI/CD Pipelines** – Add a step to generate **Dilithium** signatures for code releases; store keys in a quantum‑safe vault.
5. **Train Security Teams** – Conduct a workshop on lattice‑based cryptography; include threat modeling for quantum adversaries.
6. **Document Migration Plans** – Draft a **cut‑over playbook** that includes rollback procedures, stakeholder communication, and compliance evidence.

&gt; **Pro tip:** Use the **“dual‑key”** strategy—maintain both RSA and Kyber keys during the transition. This allows you to fall back instantly if a compatibility issue surfaces.
